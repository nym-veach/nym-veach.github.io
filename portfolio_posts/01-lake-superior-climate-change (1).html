Climate Change and Large Freshwater Lakes - Lake Superior Shoreline Case Study

Lake Superior in Minnesota is the largest freshwater lake by surface area in the world and is highly sensitive to the impacts of climate change. It holds approximately 10% of the global fresh surface water and is a valuable water resource.

Fresh surface water resources are important for drinking, recreation, wildlife habitat, global climate regulation, industry, agriculture, biodiversity, ground water recharge, and other ecological cycles. Lake Superior is widely studied, with a large body of scientific research to support this data analysis and exploration.

The scope of the analysis was to use data available through the NOAA CDO tool which has land-based stations available with continuous daily records. A buoy station does not always have long-term continuous data for climate analysis and was not available using this tool. Grand Marais, MN station along the shoreline was used as a land-based proxy to get near-shore lake air temperatures. In this data analysis, I explore the average annual air temperature trends from 1920 - 2020 using python and what they reveal about climate change in the Lake Superior region.

Wait, that slope is slightly negative?
The air temperatures are actually showing a cooling trend?
Does this mean climate change is a myth?
Not quite, theres more to the story.

This linear regression analysis showed a slope of 0.002C per year, which may suggest a very slight cooling trend, equivalent to about 0.2C over the past 100 years. This change is so small that it may be within local weather variability rather than indicating a true long-term cooling trend.

This case study is a good example of how lakes and large bodies of water can modulate local air temperature so that there are less significant temperature fluctuations which can impact the warming trends we see compared to other regions. Many large lakes are experiencing extreme climate events even when local air data may show little impact. However, temperatures in the entire great lakes region and further inland are rising as we would expect with climate change; as are water temperatures in Lake Superior rising faster than local air temperatures. Lake Superior is one of the fastest warming lakes in the world. Lake Superior is experiencing impacts of climate change, so why the misleading air temperatures?

The trend we see in this analysis shows the complex water-air-land relationship and how climate change can be more complex than one might think. The size of the lake acts as a thermal buffer by absorbing and storing heat; leading to less temperature fluctuations than one might expect, even with climate change. Local weather variability and global climate events like La Nina can also be a factor. A simple linear regression over a shoreline station may show little warming or even slight cooling, but this does not mean the lake or region isnt experiencing climate change.

Lake Superior is experiencing rising water temperatures, less ice cover, increasing precipitation, and changing storm patterns leading to things like algal blooms, lower oxygen levels, shoreline erosion, habitat loss, impacts on fish species, and more. This climate temperature data analysis highlights that more research is needed to determine the full effects of climate change on freshwater water resources and managing freshwater ecosystems in the coming decades.

Data analysis with air temperature can offer valuable insight into climate change but must always be considered with context, which is more complex than simple warming temperatures. This analysis on Grand Marais, MN, a shoreline station, is one example of how local variability can mask a broader climate trend. Lake Superior as a case study can help us understand the complex relationships between air, water, land, and climate.
