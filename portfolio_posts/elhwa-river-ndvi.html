Lower Elwha Klallum Tribe - Elwha River Dam Removal - Vegetation Changes NDVI Analysis using Python

Introduction

From the 1930s to the 1970s the United States experienced a dramatic increase in dam building, sometimes referred to as The Big Dam Era (Lee, 2023), for benefits such as hydropower, flood control, irrigation, and water supply. However, later in the 20th century research began to show that dams often had unforeseen consequences such as habitat loss, habitat fragmentation, species impacts such as salmon, sediment transport reduction, and social impacts like displaced communities or livelihoods. In more recent decades, with the growing evidence, dam removal is becoming more frequent in an attempt to restore the habitat, natural hydrologic flow, and sediment transport. The dams are also aging infrastructure.

A major dam removal project in WA was along the Elwha River. One of the biggest concerns for the Lower Elwha Klallum Tribe (LEKT) was the impact on salmon migration and fish passage. The Elwha River was once a thriving salmon habitat with plentiful fish, but dams can alter fish migration due to changes in sediment load, hydrologic flow, and water quality changes such as temperature, nutrients, and oxygen (U.S. Environmental Protection Agency, 2021). Salmon had been a major part of the lives of the Lower Elwha Klallum people for cultural, spiritual and physical purposes (Guarino, 2013). The LEKT played a major role in advocating for the removal of the Elwha River through lobbying Congress.

Location

The Elwha River is 45 miles originating in the Olympic Mountains running to the Strait of Juan De Fuca. There were two dams that were removed along the Elwha River, the Elwha Dam removal completed in 2012 and Glines Canyon Dam which was fully removed in 2014. This dam removal project was a huge undertaking and it was the largest in history at the time. It reopened 75 miles of salmon habitat (U.S.NOAA Fisheries, 2025).

Methods

To evaluate long-term vegetation change in the Lower Elwha Klallam Tribe (LEKT) boundary areas, this analysis used MODIS MOD13Q1 16-day NDVI data and a polygon from OpenStreetMap (OSM) to define the study boundary. First, a site map was created, and the LEKT boundary was found through OPM. Then, the NDVI raster data for the region was downloaded through NASA Earth Data. The data was then cleaned and processed for further analysis.

To explore changes over time, the NDVI temporal mean was calculated for two periods: 20082011 and 20122018, representing conditions before and after the dam removals in 2012 and 2014. The early-period mean was subtracted from the later-period mean to create a raster showing the long-term difference in vegetation health.

To account for normal regional variation unrelated to dam removal, such as seasonal patterns, precipitation fluctuations, or broader climate trends, we created an 'envelope' region outside the LEKT boundary for comparison. The July NDVI mean was calculated and plotted using hvplot for interactive features. The final graph created showed the difference in mean July NDVI and changes over time from within and outside the LEKT boundary.

Tracking Vegetation Recovery to Elwha River Dam Removal: NDVI Changes Within and Outside the Lower Elwha Klallam Tribe Boundary (20082018)

As more dams are being removed, it is important to look at changes in ecological health over time to ensure restoration of the rivers health. This analysis of NDVI data within the Lower Elwha Klallam Tribe (LEKT) boundary revealed modest changes in vegetation over the 2008-2018 following the removal of the Elwha and Glines Canyon Dams. We do see a possible decrease in vegetation health outside the boundary which could be due to things like changes in sediment changes at the mouth of the river or urban development. While the dataset showed limited NDVI increases on tribal lands at the mouth of the river specifically, other studies and field observations indicate that vegetation recovery is occurring elsewhere in the larger watershed. This limited change near shore is consistent with other findings that saw relatively little vegetation change at the mouth of the river in the first 3 years following dam removal (Foley et al., 2017). We do see a possible increase in vegetation health towards the end of the timeframe explored, meaning that there could be more changes if looking further into the future.

Several factors likely explain the relatively small NDVI changes observed within the LEKT boundary. Vegetation recovery often occurs gradually over time, and the strongest responses are expected in areas that were previously submerged, eroded, or otherwise impacted by the dams. These locations may not be within the LEKT analyzed along the river mouth in this dataset. Additionally, natural variation, climate influences, and the substantial pre-existing vegetation cover in that area can mask short-term NDVI responses in remote sensing data.

During this analysis and project, a key takeaway is that post-dam vegetation recovery is not spatially homogenous across the watershed and could use further study following dam removals (Shafroth, Rosi, & Pess, 2024). The Elwha River is one of the most well-studied dam removal projects in the world, yet there is still a lot to be understood. The largest increases are likely to occur in regions directly influenced by restored hydrology and sediment transport rather than uniformly across the watershed or at the mouth of the river on the tribal lands. This suggests that when evaluating ecological restoration such as vegetation health with remote sensing data, it is important to carefully select areas most likely to experience change, which can provide a more complete picture of recovery. Future analysis could use different polygon data to work with the larger watershed and see changes across the entire watershed.

Additional Reflection

This analysis required downloading NDVI data from the NASA Earthdata portal, and a lesson learned is that data requests can take several hours to process depending on the size of the dataset. Because of this time constraint, I narrowed my analysis window to the years 20082018, which provided only a few years before and after the dam removals. In reality, vegetation recovery after major sediment and hydrologic changes often takes longer to appear, so a larger temporal dataset could reveal more pronounced trends. I would be interested to analyze a larger dataset in the future. My recommendation for downloading data from NASA earth data is to submit your request before you want to analyze the data so it is ready for you to begin your analysis. Just practical note and consideration in replication of this process.
